Status: Complete

## TL;DR
- The Backrooms concept crystallized on 4chan /x/ in May 2019 (thread + reply text about “noclipping out of reality”).
- The strongest currently available archival trail for origin is: 4plebs thread archive -> spread to Reddit/Twitter -> later codified in KYM/Wikipedia.
- The iconic image’s real-world provenance has a strong evidence chain (HobbyTown Oshkosh renovation photos, 2002/2003 context, later released to Internet Archive).
- Community growth split into multiple canon styles: minimalist liminal dread vs. expanded level/entity mythos.
- Kane Pixels’ 2022 found-footage adaptation was the major mainstream acceleration point.

## Key Findings

### 1) Origin and core text
The commonly cited origin is a 4chan /x/ thread in May 2019 (“disquieting images that just feel off”), with a reply that introduced the now-famous Backrooms noclip description.

### 2) Why it resonated
The concept maps strongly to liminal-space anxiety: familiar architecture stripped of people, orientation, and purpose. The horror is existential dislocation and spatial uncertainty more than direct confrontation.

### 3) Fandom evolution
As it spread (especially on Reddit and community wikis), lore expanded into:
- “Levels”
- “Entities”
- survival-rule frameworks
This increased remixability (games, videos, episodic stories), but also caused authenticity disputes.

### 4) Canon fragmentation
There is no single canonical authority across Backrooms communities. Broadly:
- minimalist/atmospheric branch (focus on emotional liminality)
- maximalist branch (taxonomy of levels/entities/factions)
- adaptation-specific branches (e.g., Kane Pixels continuity)

### 5) Kane Pixels effect
Kane Pixels’ “The Backrooms (Found Footage)” (Jan 2022) transformed the concept from text/image folklore into widely consumed analog horror language.
